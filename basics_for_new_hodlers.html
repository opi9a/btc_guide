THE BITCOIN SYSTEM - ITS ALL ABOUT TRANSACTIONS


Bitcoin consists of a public ledger or database of transactions - known as the "blockchain" because transactions are grouped into blocks when they are validated and added to the ledger.


Bitcoins exist only by their association with transactions.


For example, every block that is added to the blockchain creates new bitcoins (currently 6.25), and it does this with a transaction sending 6.25 btc from nowhere to whoever successfully created the block. The recipient now owns those 6.25 bitcoin.


Subsequent transactions refer back to previous transactions. For example if the recipient of the 6.25 bitcoin above wanted to send 3 bitcoin to someone else, they would make a new transaction referring to the previous one in which they received the 6.25 bitcoin.


So, owning bitcoins means being a recipient of a bitcoin transaction.



ADDRESSES DEFINE THE RECIPIENTS OF A TRANSACTIONS


A transaction needs something to define where (or who) it's going to. Just like a cheque needs a recipient name, or a bank transfer needs a destination account.


In bitcoin this is defined by an "address" - a long-ish string of letters and digits (which actually just represent a very large number).


An example of a bitcoin address is: 3QjKms5ox5Df6poH7s1bBzrVKaMkvoHyGV


All bitcoin transactions have a receiving address.


Your wallet software will allow you to generate addresses that you can give to people who are sending you bitcoin.


For example, when withdrawing from an exchange you will probably paste an address from your wallet into the exchange's web page or app. Funds sent to that address will show up in your wallet.


Similar to a bank account number, the address defines who owns the bitcoin - but you need more than just the number to control the funds.


Unlike a bank account number, you normally use a different address for each transaction you receive.



PRIVATE KEYS


When bitcoin is moved in a transaction, you also need some way of verifying that this is with the consent of the owner. Otherwise everyone could steal everyone's bitcoin.


In bitcoin transactions are verified by a digital signature.


This is similar to the signature on a cheque, or a password on a banking app - but a bit more complicated because you can't just add the same signature or password to each transaction. All transactions are public, and its important that they can be validated by everybody - so a simple signature or password would just be copied and used to steal all the rest of your bitcoin.


Instead a system is used where each receiving address is associated with a secret "private key" which can generate a unique digital signature when a transaction is made using bitcoin from that address.


Everyone can check the signature is valid, and could only be generated using the private key associated with the address - but nobody can actually work out what the private key is, or use the signature to do anything else.


Private keys are very sensitive. If anyone has access to the private key associated with addresses in your wallet then they can steal any bitcoin associated with those addresses.


Hardware wallets like Trezor are designed to generate and store private keys and use them to sign transactions in a very secure way. You will never normally see a private key (though they are also just large numbers represented by letters and digits).



SEED WORDS


If your wallet has received 5 transactions, this means that each of 5 addresses in the wallet will be associated with some quantity of bitcoin - and there will be 5 corresponding private keys, one for each.


In the early days of bitcoin this was all you got. A wallet had lists of potentially large numbers of separate addresses and private keys, and that's all there was to it.


There's nothing wrong with this in principle, but it leads to some practical difficulties which actually pose big security problems.


For example if you want to make a back up of your wallet (in case the computer it's stored on breaks) then you have to copy the whole thing - all the addresses and private keys. You are really just limited to doing this with a computer - as there is so much information, and a single error could be catastrophic.


This is bad because computers can be compromised, particularly if they connect to the internet. It would be much better to be able to make inert physical backups, like on a piece of paper - which you could be sure no hacker could ever get to.


The need for a simple way to back up an entire wallet led to the modern invention of "seed words" or "mnemonic seeds".


In this system, each wallet begins by generating a large random number - which can be represented by a series of 12 (sometimes 24) words. It's easy for the user to write this sequence of words down, or ever memorise it.


Then the wallet uses clever maths to generate a series (essentially infinite) of addresses and corresponding private keys. These can be used normally, as shown above.


But the important thing is: the way these addresses and keys are generated is completely reproducible. That means that all you need is the 12 words, and you can always use those to regenerate your whole wallet. Most wallet programs use the same convention, and the maths used is all published, so a wallet can be fully recovered even if the particular software or device you used originally no longer exists.



STORING SEED WORDS


As a user of a hardware wallet - or any wallet with significant funds - the most important task by far is looking after your seed words.


This guide won't give specific advice on how to do that - it's dependent on your circumstances and preferences. But here are some approaches that have been used by other people as part of their security strategy.


Overall it's worth noting that there are two threats to worry about:


- the risk of LOSS of your seed words, so that you cannot recover your funds;

- the risk of THEFT of your seed words, so that someone else recovers and steals your funds.


These threats are usually in opposition, in the sense that a strategy which reduces risk of loss (such as having multiple copies) will often increase the risk of theft. Striking a balance between these is up to you.


Paper wallets:


The most basic level of security is to copy down your seed words on a bit of paper, and take care of it.


Multiple paper copies:


An obvious way to mitigate the risk of loss is to make multiple copies of your seed, kept in different locations. However this will likely increase the risk of theft - so you'd probably only want to do this if you had a few very secure locations.


Durable storage media:


Paper is obviously susceptible to fire and flood, so some people like to record their seed words on something more durable. Home-made solutions are obviously possible involving steel plates and punches, but kits specifically designed for the purpose are also available.


Note that the words that form the seed are deliberately designed to be uniquely defined by their first 4 letters. So if GRUEL is in the word list, it means there are no other words beginning grue (and gruesome will NOT be in the list). That means you only need to record the first 4 letters of each word, which can reduce the difficulty of this approach a lot.
